01 — Noticing & Naming the Geometry

How Shape Creates
Movement

One everyday object that demonstrates interesting geometry is a fan blade. Fans are found in homes, classrooms, offices, and public spaces, yet most people rarely think about why their blades are shaped the way they are. When I began observing them closely, I noticed that fan blades are not just flat pieces of plastic or metal. Instead, they have carefully designed curves, angles, and proportions.

What caught my attention first was how the blades appear slightly twisted along their length. The part of the blade near the center of the fan is angled differently from the outer tip. This twist is not random — it helps the fan move air more efficiently as it spins. The inner part may have a steeper angle to scoop air, while the outer edge is flatter to push the air outward more smoothly.

Another thing I noticed is that the blades form a circular pattern around a central hub. When the fan rotates, the blades repeat around the center in a balanced arrangement. Most fans use three, four, or five blades spaced evenly around the center. A fan with four blades divides the circle into four 90° sections; five blades give 72° sections. This rotational symmetry keeps the fan balanced and prevents wobbling.

"To change the shape of a blade is to change the shape of the air itself — geometry becomes force."

Looking closer, the edges of many blades are slightly curved rather than straight. This curvature helps guide airflow smoothly instead of pushing it in a harsh, uneven way. Smooth curves guide airflow in a controlled direction instead of letting it scatter randomly. Through these geometric principles, engineers can design fans that are more efficient, quieter, and more stable — showing that geometry is not just abstract mathematics. It plays a real role in engineering, physics, and everyday design.

02 — Blade Morphology

Three Geometries,
Three Philosophies

Each blade shape represents a different engineering trade-off between lift, drag, noise, manufacturing cost, and airflow direction. None is universally superior — each is optimal for its intended purpose. My design adds a fourth: the spiral.

Shape 01
Straight
Uniform chord · Zero sweep · Low twist

The straight blade is the original fan geometry — a flat rectangular profile swept around a hub. Simple, affordable, and predictable. The chord remains constant from root to tip and the pitch is uniform throughout, making it the easiest to manufacture but the least efficient aerodynamically.

Low Cost · Predictable
Shape 02
Twisted / Airfoil
Variable pitch · Constant lift · Reduced stall

The pitch angle decreases from root to tip so that every point along the blade meets the air at the optimal angle — even though the tip moves much faster than the root. Combined with an airfoil cross-section, this produces maximum lift across the full span. It is the geometry of propellers and high-efficiency fans.

High Efficiency · Engineering Peak
Shape 03
Curved / Swept
Backward sweep · Directed flow · Noise-optimised

The swept blade curves in the plane of rotation, staggering the blade's interaction with turbulent wake. This dramatically reduces tonal noise — the periodic "whoosh" of a fan. The curvature also directs airflow. Common in ceiling fans and HVAC systems where acoustics and comfort both matter.

Low Noise · Directed Flow
Shape 04
Spiral / Concentric
Logarithmic form · Expanding radius · Nature-inspired

My own design concept: blades following a spiral pattern expanding outward like a shell or galaxy. The curvature guides airflow gradually outward, naturally accelerating it. The angle gradient θ(r) = θ₀ − k·r ensures pitch adapts to rotational speed at every radius. Blades are spaced 36° apart for 10-arm balance. Nature's most efficient expansion geometry.

Creative · Nature-Inspired
03 — Geometric Close-ups

The Details
That Matter

Zooming into the geometry reveals the engineering decisions embedded in the form. The leading edge radius, the trailing edge taper, the twist distribution along the span — each feature is the visible signature of an aerodynamic choice.

The leading edge determines how cleanly air splits around the blade. The trailing edge controls vortex shedding and noise. The airfoil cross-section converts motion into pressure difference — the fundamental mechanism of all lift.

Leading Edge Radius

Rounded leading edge prevents flow separation at moderate angles of attack. Sharper is faster but stalls sooner.

Airfoil Cross-section

Max thickness at ~30% chord, smooth camber line, sharp trailing edge. This asymmetry is the source of lift.

Trailing Edge Taper

Thin trailing edge minimises pressure-recovery distance, reducing turbulent wake noise downstream.

Twist Distribution — Root to Tip

Pitch angle decreases from hub (high pitch) to tip (low pitch) to maintain consistent angle of attack despite varying velocity at each radial station.

Why it matters

The outer tip of a blade travels much faster than the root. Without twist, the tip would push air at the wrong angle — generating drag instead of lift. Twist corrects this by giving each section exactly the right pitch for its speed.

The maths

The ideal twist follows: tan(φ) = V_axial / (ω·r). As radius r increases, the denominator grows, so φ decreases. Each cross-section shown above is a blade slice — notice how the angle flattens progressively toward the tip.

In my spiral fan design, this principle extends along a curved path. The pitch still decreases with radius — but radius itself increases continuously along the spiral arm, so the twist is built naturally into the geometry. The blade becomes the twist.

Hub Geometry

Wide root handles the bending stress from centrifugal force at high RPM.

Blade Tip Winglet

Tip winglets reduce the vortex spilling from high to low pressure — a key source of noise and lost efficiency.

04 — The Mathematics

Geometry Becomes
Calculation

Fan blade geometry can be described and optimised through a small set of elegant equations. These relationships connect physical dimensions to the forces and flows the blade produces.

Swept Area
The circle of influence
A = π · R²

The full circular disc swept by rotating blades. Doubling blade radius quadruples swept area — which is why large, slow fans move more air than small, fast ones for the same energy input.

Tip Speed
Velocity at the edge
V_tip = 2π · (RPM/60) · R

The blade tip travels faster than any other part. At 800 RPM with a 20cm blade, the tip moves at over 16 m/s. Tip speed determines aerodynamic forces and noise generation — via the squared relationship.

Blade Area
The surface doing the work
A_blade ≈ chord · R

Blade area is the simplest proxy for aerodynamic surface available. Wider blades (larger chord) or longer blades (larger R) both increase this area and the pressure difference generated per revolution.

Lift Proxy
Force from geometry
L ≈ C · A_blade · V_tip²

Force scales with velocity squared — doubling RPM quadruples the lift proxy. C encodes the airfoil coefficient, air density, and pitch angle. This explains why small RPM changes have such large effects on both performance and noise.

The equations above are simplifications. Real fan blade aerodynamics requires the Navier-Stokes equations — partial differential equations describing viscous fluid flow in three dimensions. These equations have no general analytical solution, which is why modern fan design relies on computational fluid dynamics simulations.

But the simplified equations capture the essential physics. The squared relationship between speed and force explains why small increases in RPM produce large increases in noise and power consumption — and why large-diameter, slow fans are more efficient per unit of airflow than small, fast ones.

The pitch angle introduces trigonometry: the effective component of velocity producing lift is V·cos(θ), while drag is V·sin(θ). The optimal pitch that maximises lift-to-drag ratio depends on the airfoil profile — for a NACA 0012 profile, it is roughly 8–12 degrees. Below this, the blade generates insufficient pressure. Above it, flow separates and the blade stalls.

For my spiral fan design, the angle gradient θ(r) = θ₀ − k·r ensures pitch decreases linearly as radius increases — so even as outer sections of the spiral move faster, the angle of attack stays within the efficient operating range. The spiral geometry itself comes from nature: shells, galaxies, and hurricanes all follow logarithmic spirals because this shape allows a system to expand outward while maintaining structural integrity and continuous curvature.

Pitch Twist Equation
tan(φ) = V_axial / (ω · r)

φ = pitch angle at radius r. Defines the ideal twist distribution for uniform angle of attack along the blade span.

Spiral Angle Gradient
θ(r) = θ₀ − k·r

θ = blade angle, r = distance from center, k = rate of change. Governs the pitch variation along the spiral blade's expanding radius.

Power Coefficient
Cp = P / (½ρAV³)

The Betz limit caps Cp at 0.593 — no blade can extract more than 59.3% of kinetic energy from a fluid stream. A fundamental law of aerodynamics.

06 — Significance

Why This Geometry
Changes the World

Fan blade geometry is embedded in the infrastructure of modern civilisation — from the fans cooling data centres to the turbines generating wind power. The blade shape is not decoration. It is a physical argument about fluid dynamics.

Energy Efficiency

The shape of a fan blade directly determines how much electrical energy is converted to useful airflow — and how much is wasted as heat, noise, and turbulence. A well-designed twisted, profiled blade can achieve 40–60% higher efficiency than a simple flat blade of identical dimensions.

In a world where buildings account for roughly 40% of global energy consumption and HVAC systems account for half of that, blade geometry is an energy policy issue. The difference between well-designed and poorly-designed blades, multiplied across billions of fans worldwide, represents a significant fraction of global electricity consumption.

Acoustic Engineering

The geometry of a blade determines its acoustic signature. Straight blades produce a sharp, periodic sound as each blade passes through the same air column. Swept and curved blades distribute this interaction in time and space, producing a broader, quieter sound spectrum.

This is why premium ceiling fans use swept, profiled blades rather than flat rectangular ones. The acoustic design is inseparable from the geometric design — quietness is a shape. The curvature is not decorative; it is the mechanism by which noise energy is redistributed into inaudibility.

Wind Turbines

Modern turbine blades are the most aerodynamically refined objects humans manufacture at scale — twisted, tapered, profiled for variable wind.

Data Centres

Server cooling fans run 24/7 at millions of sites. Their blade geometry directly affects global electricity consumption.

Drone Propellers

UAV propellers balance thrust, efficiency, and noise in a small package — the same equations as industrial fans, miniaturised.

HVAC Systems

Building climate systems move billions of cubic metres of air daily. Blade geometry determines energy cost and occupant comfort.

Through this project I realized that even simple objects like fans depend on careful geometric design. Angles control airflow. Symmetry creates balance. Curves guide motion. My spiral fan design explores how geometry can be both creative and functional — not just two separate things.

A fan blade is a physical argument — an object that embodies a claim about the behaviour of fluids. The straight blade says: simplicity is a virtue. The twisted blade says: every radial station demands its own response. The spiral blade says: nature's geometry is its own justification.

I hope viewers of this project begin to notice the hidden mathematics in everyday objects and realize that geometry quietly shapes the world around us.
